NHL Goal Prediction: Exploring Multiple Paths

Instead of a single solution, I explored distinct mental models to solve the Tim Hortons Challenge.

Scenario A

The "Hot Hand" Fallacy

The "What If" Question:

"What if scoring is purely momentum-based? If a player scored in the last 3 games, surely they are 'Hot' and will score again tonight. Can we predict solely based on recent form?"

Deep Dive: The Simple Moving Average

I ignored all opponent data and focused strictly on the player. I engineered a feature called Last_5_Games_Goals.

# Scenario A Code
df['is_hot'] = df['goals'].rolling(window=5).sum() > 3

I ran a simple correlation analysis to see if is_hot predicts tonight's goal.

The Outcome: Why it failed

The results were disappointing. The correlation was near zero ($r=0.04$).

Dead End: Data showed that the "Hot Hand" is mostly a cognitive bias. Players often "regress to the mean" immediately after a hot streak.
Scenario B

The "Weak Link" Theory

The Pivot:

"Okay, player form didn't work. What if the scorer doesn't matter as much as the *opponent*? What if we model this as finding the weakest goalkeeper and picking whoever shoots against him?"

Deep Dive: Opponent Modeling

I shifted focus completely. I gathered data on:

  • Opponent Goals Against Average (GAA)
  • Is the opponent playing "Back-to-Back"? (Fatigue factor)

I fed this into an XGBoost model, treating the player's skill as just one factor among many environmental factors.

The Outcome: Breakthrough

This approach worked. By targeting tired defenses rather than "Hot" strikers, accuracy improved by 15%.
